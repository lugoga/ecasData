1  Management Context

The authority to manage coastal and marine fisheries in Tanzania is legally under the Ministry of Livestock and Fisheries. The marine fisheries in Tanzania is dominated by small scale artisanal fisheries that constitute more than 90 percent of the total landed weights. The Tanzania Fisheries Research Institute (TAFIRI) is the public institute with delegated authority from the ministry to conduct research of fisheries and related field in the freshwater and marine waters of Tanzania. The fisheries of Tanzania are generally defined as fishing activities that take place in the United Republic of territorial and Exclusive Economic Zones—up to 200 nautical miles from it’s coastline. WWF-Tanzania is actively collaborating with public agencies to ensure fisheries resources are utilized and sustained for future generations.

In 2018, TAFIRI in collaboration with WWF conducted a pilot study to explore the use of the mobile application as a tool in fisheries data collection (Sailale & Mpuruti, 2021). The initiative started with five selected villages and members from village’s BMU were trained to use an application to record fisheries data in the electronic Catch Assessment Survey (e-CAS). Through this initiative several species of tuna and tuna-like were recorded for the first time (Kuguru and Sailale, 2018). Following the benefits of the e-CAS, the Government of Tanzania adopted the platform and endorsed as tool for fisheries data collection both in coastal and marine waters. Later, the East African Community (EAC) through Lake Victoria Fisheries Organization (LVFO) Secretariat approved the use of e–CAS to collect and manage fisheries resources in the area.

Today, amount of fisheries data that exists in the e–CAS are enough to assist scientist to evaluate and explore the fisheries dynamics in both coastal and freshwater ecosystems. Recognizing the archived fisheries data, early 2019 WWF TCO/WWF UK requested TAFIRI to assess fisheries performance in coastal areas that receive WWF’s support and compare the findings with coastal areas without WWF’s support. Early 2020, WWF TCO through the support of Blue Action Fund (BAF) commissioned a consultancy work to TAFIRI to extend the analysis and of fisheries in the e-CAS that was collected between 2017 and 2020.

BAF aims to strengthen small scale fisheries management of three priority groups—octopus, small pelagic and prawn in the Kibiti, Mafia and Kilwa seascape. Given the size of the area, BAF strategically chose ten collaborative fisheries managed areas (CFMAs) that bring together 42 villages that intersect with the proposed Man and Biosphere Reserve. BAF focus on monitoring fisheries resources in the area and ensure sustainable utilization of fisheries and its related resources. The project prime goal is to determine biological and exploitation fisheries reference points that can be used as indicators to gauge the state of fisheries in the area.

Though the e–CAS has archieved a substantial amount of artisal fish records, however, these data are fishery dependent and lack important measurements like total length and weight along with maturity stage of individual fish. These three measurements are key to assess the stock of data poor fishery like those in the Kibiti, Mafia, and Kilwa Seascape. With additional data collected through SWIOFISH project, the data in e–CAS can suffice to assess the stock of some species and serve as reference points for fisheries exploitation in the future.

1.1 Scope of the Assignment

This assignment intends to improve the accuracy of the artisanal fisheries catch data that has been collected through an electronic Catch Assessment Survey (e–CAS). The information generated through this study will assist fisheries managers and decision makers to plan and allocate resources for strategic intervations that are based on scientific findings. Furthermore, the assignment explored the strength and weakness of the platform and identify gaps that need to be addressed in order to refine and produce a quality report in the future.

1.2 Objectives

The overall objective of this assignment was use archived fisheries catch data in e–CAS, analyse them and reports of the fishery state in Kibiti, Mafia and Kilwa coastal area. Understanding that will help to improve the accuracy of the artisanal fisheries catch data to better inform policy and decision making for improved management of small-scale fisheries resources in the RUMAKI seascape. More specifically, the specific objectives were:

  1. Provide clear definition of the research guiding questions for undertaking the study based on the assigned TORs.
  2. Analyse quality of the available e-CAS data for priority fisheries and other fisheries from ten (10) CFMAs landing sites in Rufiji/Kibiti, Mafia and Kilwa.
  3. Determine spatial distribution of priority fisheries and fishing effort in the project area
  4. Undertake comparison of catch per unit effort (CPUE) and total production in the project area (Rufiji, Mafia and Kilwa Districts) per district from 2010 – 2018 as a baseline reference point.
  5. Using historical fisheries data from MIMP (2010-2018) to analyse CPUE, species composition and fish production trends of priority fisheries (CAS in MIMP 2010-2018; e-CAS 2017-2020, CAS from fisheries data base (2010-2015) in Rufiji, Mafia and Kilwa districts)
  6. Define a simple and replicable indicator and baseline on the sustainability of 3 priority fisheries (Small Pelagics, Prawn and Octopus) focusing on the CFMAs in the districts of Mafia, Kibiti and Kilwa (e.g. CPUE, total production), based on available data.
  7. Develop data driven tools to complement existing statistical system and recommend on how to improve;
  8. Prepare a policy brief in close collaboration with WWF marine programme staff based on analysed information to inform decision-makers and stakeholders about the status and sustainability of fisheries in Rufiji-Mafia-Kilwa seascape
